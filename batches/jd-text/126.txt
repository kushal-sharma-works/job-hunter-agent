Applying for jobs is hard already.

It can be even harder when your lifestyle looks a little different.

And, when companies see "Bogotá" on an application, I imagine it raises some questions.

Or Portugal.

Or Mexico.

I could just put New York.

But I'm not really one for lying. (Also, a lot of ya'll are doing this and your companies never catch you/it's also never been a problem until it is a problem. Still, I prefer to be transparent.)

I've been working remotely from abroad for more than a decade. I have a U.S. LLC, I'm a U.S. citizen, I pay U.S. taxes, and I've worked with companies of all sizes this way.

I also completely understand that some roles genuinely need someone physically in the U.S.

But for jobs that are 100% remote, I'm genuinely curious where the hesitation comes from.

If someone is engaged as an independent contractor, there are established ways to do this. There are W-9s, W-8s (not for U.S. citizens!), 1099s, Employer of Record services, and other options depending on the situation.

Not only this, though. In many cases, there are actually a lot of benefits to hiring someone who is abroad. Because, companies can also avoid costs like:

- Health insurance and other employee benefits
- Payroll taxes associated with employees
- Equipment stipends or office overhead
- Long-term employment commitments

(though, stipends are usually appreciated!)

I'm not a tax attorney or an HR expert. (Although I am the granddaughter of a CPA who always joked I was his most complicated client.)
So this is a genuine question for recruiters, founders, and HR folks:

What is the biggest concern when you see a qualified candidate living abroad?

Is it compliance? Taxes? Insurance? Company policy? Something else?

And, for companies that don't have a policy and can hire globally...why? How do you do it? What are the challenges?

I'd love to know!
